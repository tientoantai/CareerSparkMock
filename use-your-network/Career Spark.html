Make the Connection

  • Your most valuable job search tool is already in your possession: your personal and professional network. Employee referrals make up 40 percent of all hires. So, get out there and make your network work for you.
  • Today, people switch jobs, and even careers, more frequently than in the past. You can take advantage of these job changes by building and maintaining relationships with people you have worked with, volunteered with and been acquainted with over the years.

Reach out and find a career.

Working Your Network

Make sure your current network knows you are looking for employment and what type of position you’re looking for.

How do you reach your most valuable asset? Pick an outlet, any outlet. Reach out to your network through Facebook, LinkedIn, email and many more. A 10-minute, face-to-face meeting to reconnect, learn more about the field they’re in, and let them know what you’re looking for is ideal. Be specific. Discuss the industry you’d like to work in, the specific company and even the role you’re seeking. If a sit-down meeting is not feasible, a written exchange will be enough to maintain the relationship. You get to rekindle an old friendship while getting closer to your new job. It’s a win-win.

Tips for Expanding Your Network

Your network is your No. 1 tool to finding a job and the support system that will continually advance you throughout your career. Expanding your network takes time, but your patience and persistence will pay off professionally in the end.

1 Get out of your comfort zone

Attend an organized event or conference, join associations, volunteer your time, and attend social meetings you might not normally attend. At any event, set a goal of talking with two to three people with whom you share a common interest or a connection. It’s less stress and more fun, and it gets you home earlier than trying to meet everyone in the room. Don’t forget to collect a business card or contact information from everyone you meet!

2 Network a little every day

Try to achieve one networking task a day. This keeps networking from being an overwhelming responsibility and more of a daily habit that will ultimately strengthen your network. Send an email to a new contact from that event last week, introduce yourself to a neighbor, or call someone you haven’t heard from in a while.

3 Take a balanced approach

Leverage networking opportunities both online and in person. Online sites and tools are convenient and effective, but it’s equally important to attend local networking events in person and connect face to face. Always be polite and professional, and ask your contacts how they prefer to stay in touch!

4 It’s a two-way street

Remember, it’s not all about you! Networking is an exchange of information, so make yourself a valuable resource to your contacts. What does your contact do? What challenges does he or she face? Try to draw from your experiences and make yourself useful by providing a solution.
