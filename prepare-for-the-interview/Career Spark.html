Prepare to Shine

  • The interview. Perhaps the most nerve wracking part of the entire process. We’ve all been there; it’s only human to feel nervous and unsure. But good news! You can feel prepared and confident by knowing what to expect. Review the top 10
  • interview questions and sample answers below. You could even practice writing down your answers ahead of time. No one’s going to blame you for being prepared, plus you’ll seem quick on your feet and confident during the process.

  • 1. Tell me about yourself.

    Be brief! 30–45 seconds max or you will lose the employer’s attention very quickly. Discuss your strongest skills right off the bat with a statement like the sample answer below. You indicated earlier that a few strong skills you can talk about are .

    Sample Answer: “My name is Cynthia Murphy. I am a mother of two with a teaching degree from Oklahoma University. I’ve taught at an elementary school for the past four years and volunteered at childcare centers in my community. I’ve gained exceptional organizational and leadership skills during my time as a teacher, and strengthened my motivational skills during my volunteering at childcare centers.”

    Remember, the interviewer doesn’t want your life story; simply summarize your skills and experience in a way that makes you stand out. Show why you are the best person for the job by being prepared with a more detailed story for each skill—how you used and strengthened them at past jobs or while volunteering.

  • 2. What about this job interests you?

    The interviewer is listening for an answer that indicates you’ve given this some thought and are not sending out resumes just because there is an opening. Be clear about why you are interested in the job and the value you can bring to the organization.

    Sample Answer: “This job is really attractive to me because it combines my interest and experience in teaching with the opportunity to nurture, learn and grow by teaching children with special needs.”

  • 3. What are your career goals and where do you see yourself five years from now?

    Don’t feel the need to address future PCS moves here. The key here is to focus on your achievable objectives and what you are doing to reach those objectives.

    Sample Answer: “I want to secure a public relations position with a national firm that concentrates on nonprofits. I would like to work for a young company, such as this one, so I can get in on the ground floor and take advantage of all the opportunities a growing firm has to offer.”

  • 4. Why did you leave your past job?

    Always focus on the positive. You relocated and are now looking for a new challenge, new responsibilities, more experience and a change in work environment. Never be negative about your former or current employers, talk about negative experiences you had there, or speak negatively about your move.

    Sample Answer: “I recently moved and I’m looking for a new challenge and new responsibilites. I’ve learned a lot about myself and what kind of environment I can thrive in. Now I know how I can contribute the most.”

  • 5. What are your strengths?

    This is one question that you know you are going to be asked, so be prepared! Concentrate on discussing your main strengths. List three or four proficiencies such as your ability to learn quickly, determination to succeed, positive attitude, ability to relate to people and achieve a common goal, etc. (Look back at the resume you created if you need a reminder!)

    Sample Answer: “I have very good organizational and time management skills, but my greatest strength is my ability to effectively handle multiple events and deadlines.”

  • 6. What are your weaknesses or areas you would like to improve?

    This is another frequently asked question. Everyone has weaknesses, but when answering this question in a job interview, stay away from personal qualities and focus on professional traits. Describe what steps you have taken to overcome your weaknesses so that you can demonstrate areas of improvement.

    Sample Answer: “Sometimes I have trouble delegating duties to others. This has sometimes backfired because I’d end up with more work than I could handle. I’ve since taken courses in time management and learned more effective delegation techniques, and I’m happy to say that my last several team projects were a great success.”

  • 7. What are your salary expectations?

    Do your research and know your bottom line. Research the industry standard for the positions you are applying for in your geographic area. Whenever possible, try to defer the salary question on the first interview so that you don’t under- or oversell yourself. If pressured, be prepared to give the employer a salary range.

    Sample Answer: “I’m sure that your company offers a fair, competitive salary for someone with my education/training, skills and experience. I am also willing to negotiate for the right position.”

    Sample Answer: “I will need more information about the job and the responsibilities before we can discuss salary, but it would be great if you could give me an idea of the salary range you have budgeted for this position.”

  • 8. Have you ever had a conflict with a superior or colleague? How did you handle it?

    Almost everyone has been in this situation, whether in an office or elsewhere. If you say no, interviewers will usually dig deeper until you can give them an answer. The key is to show how you responded to the conflict and what you did to resolve it.

    Sample Answer: “I think that everyone at some point has had a conflict at work. When I was responsible for coordinating a group of 15 committee volunteers, there were plenty of differences of opinion between the members of the team. I’ve found that when I’m in a difficult situation, it helps to communicate with the other person, understand their perspective, and try to work out a collaborative solution from there.”

  • 9. Why should we hire you?

    Be prepared to state what you bring to the organization such as your knowledge, skills, experience, education/training and personal qualities that demonstrate why you are the best person for the job. Show how you would add value to the company. Always qualify your answers with measurable results you have achieved in previous jobs or assignments.

    Sample Answer: “I think I am a great match for this position. My degree in business, coupled with more than 10 years of experience across the country managing groups of all sizes helped me to improve productivity and reduce volunteer turnover. I believe that I can do the same for your organization and would be a great addition to your team.”

  • 10. As a military spouse, why should I hire you if you may be moving again soon?

    You probably can't give the employer an exact time frame, but make them feel confident with your abilities and help them understand that another PCS move may not even be on the horizon. Try something like, "Military moves can be unpredictable, so I can't say when or even if I'll move again. But I can promise that if you hire me, I'll give you more than 100% for as long as I'm here."

  • 11. Do you have any questions?

    Always be prepared to ask the interviewer a few questions as well. This helps to demonstrate your preparation and interest.

    Sample questions might include:

    How would you describe a typical week/day in this position?
    Is this a new position? If not, what did the previous employee go on to do?
    How would you describe the company’s management style?
    Whom does this position report to? If I am offered the position, can I meet him/her?
    How many people work in this office/department?
    Is travel expected in this position? If so, how much?
    What are the prospects for growth and advancement?
    What would you say are the best things about working here?
    Would you like a list of references?
    If I am extended a job offer, how soon would you like me to start?
